Artists at the Intersection of Technology, Systems, and Critique
Hito Steyerl
German filmmaker and media artist. One of the most prominent critical voices on AI, digital capitalism, and the politics of images. Coined the term "artificial stupidity" to counter inflated claims about AI. Works include large-scale immersive installations examining surveillance, labor exploitation, and technological dependency. Her framing — that tools like ChatGPT create an illusion of agency while dependency deepens — is a useful intellectual anchor. Recent book: Medium Hot: Images in the Age of Heat (2025). Represented by Andrew Kreps Gallery.
James Bridle
British artist and writer based in Athens. Coined the "New Aesthetic" — the intersection of digital systems and physical reality. Holds a CS/cognitive science degree with a dissertation on creative applications of AI. Books: New Dark Age (2018) and Ways of Being (2022). Practice moves fluidly between writing, installation, and small precise interventions. Most useful as a model for someone building a practice from a technical background — he started from writing and specific conceptual gestures, not spectacle. Worth reading before making anything.
Survival Research Laboratories (SRL)
Founded 1978 by Mark Pauline in San Francisco. Large-scale machine performance involving custom-built robots that destroy each other in dangerous public shows. Described as socio-political satire through industrial violence. Less directly relevant than initially implied — their concern is machine violence as spectacle, not the intimate human-machine entanglement your work is pointing toward.
Ken Rinaldo
American artist and professor (Ohio State, emeritus). Works at the intersection of robotics, bioart, and AI — focused on trans-species communication, emergent systems, and human/animal/machine ecologies. Notable works: Autopoiesis (2000), Augmented Fish Reality (2004, a fish-driven robot). Interesting work but oriented toward biological systems and interspecies communication rather than institutional or AI critique. Less relevant than initially implied.
Monika Fleischmann
German media art pioneer working with partner Wolfgang Strauss since the late 1980s. Co-founded ART+COM, Germany's first digital media research institute. Notable works: Home of the Brain (1990–91), the world's first VR artwork; Liquid Views (1992), an interactive touchscreen installation. Important historically — she invented the touchscreen to make her art — but the work is from a moment when interactivity itself was the content. Less useful as a contemporary reference.
Tega Brain (most relevant)
Australian artist and environmental engineer, currently at NYU. Degrees in environmental engineering and fine arts. Practice examines ecology, automation, data, and infrastructure through what she calls "eccentric engineering" — purposefully dysfunctional systems that critique the values embedded in technological design. Key insight: system failures and leaks redistribute power in ways optimized systems suppress. Notable works: Solar Protocol (2021), Synthetic Messenger (2021), Unfit Bits (2015). Book: Code as Creative Medium (MIT Press, with Golan Levin). Fellow at Eyebeam and Data & Society. Start here. Read her essay "The Environment is not a System."
